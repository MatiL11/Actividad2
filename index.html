The Futility of Rolling Stone's
Best-Albums List

By Sheldon Pearce
October 2, 2020
  • The Original List
  • What Changed
  • The Problem with Lists
  • The Internet Era
  • Conclusion

Rolling Stone released its updated list of the five hundred greatest albums of all time this week, and if you want to understand why these lists are both useful and maddening, consider that the top five albums are, in order: Marvin Gaye's "What's Going On," the Beach Boys' "Pet Sounds," Joni Mitchell's "Blue," Robert Johnson's "The Complete Recordings," and Aretha Franklin's "I Never Loved a Man the Way I Love You." That's three Black artists and two white ones—an improvement over the 2003 version of the list, in which all five albums in the top five were by white artists.

The 2003 list was an artifact of its time: a document that reflected the biases and blind spots of a particular critical establishment. It was dominated by rock music—specifically, the guitar-driven rock of the nineteen-sixties and seventies—and it was almost entirely male. Of the five hundred albums, fewer than forty were by women or groups with female members. Hip-hop, which had spent two decades reshaping American music, was barely represented.

What Changed

In the years since, Rolling Stone has expanded its pool of voters. The new list was compiled from the votes of more than three hundred musicians, producers, critics, and industry figures—a broader group than before. The changes show: hip-hop now accounts for more than fifty entries on the list. Albums by women have more than doubled. The top spot went to a Black artist for the first time.

These are meaningful shifts. The problem is that they feel less like a genuine reckoning with the past and more like a correction—an attempt to right old wrongs without fully interrogating why those wrongs existed in the first place. The list's logic remains largely intact: it still privileges albums that have stood the test of time, as measured by their influence on subsequent generations of musicians and critics.

The Problem with Lists

There is an inherent tension in any best-of list, and it has to do with the difference between importance and quality. A song or album can be enormously important—historically significant, culturally transformative—without being particularly pleasurable to listen to. Conversely, something can be a joy to experience while leaving almost no mark on the broader culture.

Rolling Stone has always tried to have it both ways, valuing both quality and influence, and the result is a list that sometimes feels less like a celebration of music than a syllabus for a survey course in rock history. This is not a bad thing, exactly—there is value in canons, in having a shared set of reference points—but it does limit the list's usefulness as a guide to what's actually worth listening to.

"The canon is not a neutral document. It is a record of who has had the power to define what matters."

The Internet Era

The deeper problem is that the very concept of a single, authoritative list of the greatest albums feels increasingly out of step with the way people actually experience music. The Internet has shattered the old gatekeeping structures, making it possible for any artist to reach a global audience without the approval of major labels or mainstream critics. Streaming services have made virtually all recorded music available to anyone with a smartphone, collapsing the distinctions between genres and eras.

In this environment, the idea of a definitive canon seems not just unnecessary but actively counterproductive. Lists like this one inevitably reflect the tastes of a particular group of people at a particular moment in time. They are, by definition, exclusionary. Every album that makes the list implicitly claims that the thousands of albums that didn't make it are less worthy of attention.

Streaming and Discovery

Streaming has also changed the way we discover music. Algorithms now do much of the work that critics once did, surfacing new artists based on listening habits rather than cultural authority. This has democratized music discovery in some ways—anyone can stumble across an obscure Malian guitarist or a bedroom-pop singer-songwriter from New Zealand—but it has also created new kinds of bubbles, new forms of homogeneity.

Conclusion

None of this means that the Rolling Stone list is worthless. It is a useful document, a snapshot of where the critical establishment stands in 2020, and it will no doubt introduce a new generation of listeners to albums they might otherwise have missed. But it is worth remembering what it is and what it isn't. It is not a map of all the great music that has ever been made. It is not an objective ranking. It is an opinion, arrived at by a particular group of people using a particular set of criteria.

The needle is moving, even if it isn't yet reflecting the speed and lawlessness of the Internet. That's something. But the real revolution in music listening isn't happening on a list. It's happening everywhere else.
